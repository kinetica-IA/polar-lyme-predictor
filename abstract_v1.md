## Abstract (250 words)

**Background:** Post-Lyme disease syndrome and related post-infectious fatigue conditions are characterized by unpredictable exacerbations of symptom burden, limiting patients' ability to self-manage activity and load. Autonomic nervous system (ANS) dysfunction is a proposed pathophysiological mechanism, but whether ANS signals can serve as early warning indicators has not been tested in ecological, daily-life settings using consumer-grade wearables.

**Objective:** To determine whether nocturnal ANS metrics derived from a consumer wearable predict high-severity symptom days 24–48 hours in advance in a single patient with post-Lyme fatigue.

**Methods:** Retrospective N-of-1 study. ANS status, nocturnal RMSSD, and recovery indicator were extracted from six months of Polar Grit X2 nightly data (185 days). Subjective symptom burden (seven domains, 0–10 scale) was captured via a structured daily diary across 43 days (Sep-Oct 2025 + Feb 2026). Spearman correlations were computed at lag 0–3 days. A logistic regression model (L2, C=0.5) with leave-one-out cross-validation was trained on 34 effective observation pairs to predict high-severity days (≥7/10).

**Results:** ANS status at lag-2 showed the strongest prospective association with severe symptom burden (ρ=+0.431, p=0.011, n=34). LOO-CV AUC-ROC was 0.656 (sensitivity=81.2%, specificity=66.7%). Sleep score showed no predictive relationship with next-day symptoms (ρ≈0). ANS status was the dominant model feature (standardized coefficient: +0.598).

**Conclusions:** Consumer-grade nocturnal ANS monitoring may provide a 48-hour early warning window for symptomatic exacerbations in post-Lyme fatigue. The signal resides in autonomic dynamics rather than sleep architecture. Replication in a prospective, multi-subject design is required.

**Keywords:** post-Lyme disease, ME/CFS, heart rate variability, autonomic nervous system, wearable technology, N-of-1, symptom prediction, digital health
